# Clinical Knowledge Base: Breast Cancer Treatment Modalities

## Multidisciplinary Approach
Breast cancer treatment is highly personalized and typically involves a combination of local therapies (targeting the breast directly) and systemic therapies (treating the entire body). A multidisciplinary tumor board consisting of surgical, medical, and radiation oncologists decides the optimal sequence of treatments.

---

## 1. Local Therapies

### Surgery
Surgery is usually the first line of defense for non-metastatic breast cancer.
- **Breast-Conserving Surgery (BCS / Lumpectomy)**: Removes only the tumor and a surrounding margin of healthy tissue. Preserves the rest of the breast. Almost always followed by radiation therapy to reduce recurrence risk.
- **Mastectomy**: Surgical removal of the entire breast tissue.
  - *Simple/Total Mastectomy*: Removes the breast tissue, nipple, and areola.
  - *Modified Radical Mastectomy*: Removes the entire breast plus underarm lymph nodes (axillary dissection).
- **Lymph Node Assessment**:
  - *Sentinel Lymph Node Biopsy (SLNB)*: Identifies and removes the first few lymph nodes to which cancer would spread. If clear, no further node removal is needed.
  - *Axillary Lymph Node Dissection (ALND)*: Removes multiple nodes if SLNB shows cancer spread.

### Radiation Therapy
Uses high-energy rays (like X-rays or protons) to destroy remaining cancer cells.
- **Adjuvant Radiation**: Recommended after a lumpectomy or, in some cases, after a mastectomy if the tumor was large or involved lymph nodes.
- **Types**:
  - *External Beam Radiation Therapy (EBRT)*: Delivered from a machine outside the body over several weeks.
  - *Brachytherapy (Internal Radiation)*: Temporary radioactive seeds or pellets placed inside the breast tissue near the tumor site.

---

## 2. Systemic Therapies

### Chemotherapy
Uses powerful drugs to target rapidly dividing cells throughout the body. Can be given in cycles.
- **Neoadjuvant Chemotherapy**: Given *before* surgery to shrink large tumors, making them easier to remove or allowing for a lumpectomy instead of a mastectomy.
- **Adjuvant Chemotherapy**: Given *after* surgery to destroy undetected micro-metastases and lower recurrence risk.
- **Common Agents**: Anthracyclines (Doxorubicin/Adriamycin), Taxanes (Paclitaxel/Taxol, Docetaxel/Taxotere), Cyclophosphamide, and Carboplatin.
- **Side Effects**: Hair loss, fatigue, nausea, increased risk of infection (neutropenia), and neuropathies.

### Endocrine (Hormone) Therapy
For hormone receptor-positive (ER+ and/or PR+) cancers. It blocks estrogen from binding to receptors or lowers estrogen levels in the body.
- **Selective Estrogen Receptor Modulators (SERMs)**:
  - *Tamoxifen*: Blocks estrogen receptors in breast cells. Used for both pre- and post-menopausal women.
- **Aromatase Inhibitors (AIs)**:
  - *Anastrozole (Arimidex), Letrozole (Femara), Exemestane (Aromasin)*: Blocks the enzyme aromatase, which converts other hormones into estrogen. **Only effective for post-menopausal women**.
- **Ovarian Suppression**: LHRH agonists (e.g., Goserelin/Zoladex) shut down the ovaries in pre-menopausal women to lower estrogen levels, often combined with an AI.

### Targeted Therapy
Specifically targets molecular alterations in cancer cells, sparing healthy tissue.
- **HER2-Targeted Drugs**: Trastuzumab (Herceptin), Pertuzumab (Perjeta), and Antibody-Drug Conjugates (ADCs) like Trastuzumab Deruxtecan (Enhertu).
- **CDK4/6 Inhibitors**: Palbociclib (Ibrance), Ribociclib (Kisqali), Abemaciclib (Verzenio). Combined with hormone therapy for hormone-receptor-positive metastatic cancers.
- **PARP Inhibitors**: Olaparib (Lynparza), Talazoparib (Talzenna). For patients with inherited BRCA1 or BRCA2 mutations.

### Immunotherapy
Helps the patient's own immune system identify and destroy cancer cells.
- **Pembrolizumab (Keytruda)**: A checkpoint inhibitor (PD-1 blocker). Used in combination with chemotherapy for high-risk early-stage or metastatic Triple-Negative Breast Cancer (TNBC).
