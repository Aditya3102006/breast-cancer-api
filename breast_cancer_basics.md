# Clinical Knowledge Base: Breast Cancer Basics

## Overview
Breast cancer is a disease in which abnormal breast cells grow out of control and form tumors. If left unchecked, these tumors can spread (metastasize) throughout the body and become life-threatening. It is the most common cancer diagnosed in women globally, though it can also occur in men.

## Classifications
Breast cancers are generally classified by where they begin, how far they have spread, and their molecular characteristics:
1. **Ductal Carcinoma in Situ (DCIS)**: A non-invasive, pre-cancerous condition where abnormal cells are found in the lining of a breast duct but have not spread outside the duct.
2. **Invasive Ductal Carcinoma (IDC)**: The most common type of breast cancer (about 80% of cases). It begins in the milk ducts and invades nearby breast tissue.
3. **Invasive Lobular Carcinoma (ILC)**: Starts in the lobules (milk-producing glands) and accounts for about 10-15% of cases.
4. **Inflammatory Breast Cancer (IBC)**: A rare, aggressive form where cancer cells block lymph vessels in the skin of the breast, causing the breast to look red, swollen, and warm.

## Common Signs and Symptoms
Early stages of breast cancer may not cause any symptoms. As the tumor grows, symptoms may include:
- A painless, hard lump or mass in the breast or underarm area (axilla). Lumps with irregular edges are more likely to be cancerous, though some can be soft and rounded.
- Swelling of all or part of a breast (even if no lump is felt).
- Skin dimpling or puckering (resembling an orange peel, known as *peau d'orange*).
- Breast or nipple pain.
- Nipple retraction (turning inward).
- Redness, scaling, flaking, or thickening of the nipple skin or breast skin.
- Nipple discharge other than breast milk (especially if bloody or spontaneous).

## Risk Factors
A risk factor is anything that increases the chance of developing breast cancer. 
- **Gender**: Being female is the primary risk factor.
- **Age**: Risk increases with age; most cases are diagnosed in women over 50.
- **Genetics**: About 5-10% of cases are hereditary. Mutations in the **BRCA1** and **BRCA2** genes significantly elevate lifetime risk.
- **Family History**: Having a first-degree relative (mother, sister, daughter) diagnosed increases risk.
- **Dense Breast Tissue**: High density makes mammograms harder to read and correlates with increased risk.
- **Reproductive History**: Early menstruation (before age 12) or late menopause (after age 55) exposes the body to estrogen longer, raising risk.
- **Lifestyle Factors**: Lack of physical activity, alcohol consumption, being overweight or obese after menopause, and radiation exposure.

## Diagnosis and Detection
Early detection increases survival rates.
- **Mammography**: X-ray of the breast, the gold standard for screening. Can detect microcalcifications or masses before they can be felt.
- **Breast Ultrasound**: Sound wave imaging used to evaluate lumps found on mammograms or physical exams, helping differentiate fluid-filled cysts (benign) from solid masses (suspicious).
- **Breast MRI**: Uses magnetic fields to generate detailed images, often recommended for high-risk patients or for determining the extent of disease after diagnosis.
- **Biopsy**: The definitive test. A needle or surgical procedure removes cells or tissue for laboratory evaluation by a pathologist.
